# Quantitative Aptitude — Teaching Tips

## Student Guide

### Where to Begin
If you are scoring below 70 percentile in Quant, start with Arithmetic — it accounts for roughly 40-45% of Quant questions and rewards systematic practice the fastest. The sequence: Percentages → Profit-Loss → Ratio → Time-Work → TSD → Mixtures.

### How to Study Each Topic
1. **Learn the core formula** (do not memorise a wall of them — know 3-4 per topic deeply)
2. **Do 5 easy problems** to build pattern recognition
3. **Do 5 medium problems** — try to spot the shortcut before calculating
4. **Do 2 hard problems** — analyse every step of the solution even if you solved it correctly
5. **Review mistakes immediately**: classify each error as: (a) concept gap, (b) calculation slip, (c) misread question

### Building Speed
- Practice mental math daily: squares up to 30, cubes up to 12, tables up to 20
- Learn fraction ↔ percentage equivalents by heart (see formula sheet)
- Time yourself on every practice session — the timer trains your internal clock

### When to Guess in Exam
- MCQ: guess if you can eliminate 2 options (expected value becomes positive)
- TITA: always attempt if you can find a range or estimate — zero negative marking
- Skip if: you have been on a question > 2.5 mins with no progress

## Teacher / Tutor Guide

### Diagnosing Student Weakness
Ask students to categorise their last 20 wrong answers:
- **Category A** (concept gap): They did not know the formula or method → teach the concept
- **Category B** (calculation error): They knew the method but made arithmetic mistakes → drill mental math
- **Category C** (misread): They solved a different version of the question → teach careful reading, especially for "least/greatest", "neither/both", "value of x²/value of x"

### Teaching Arithmetic
- Start with the **multiplier method** for percentages — it is the gateway to all arithmetic
- Example: "A increases by 25% then decreases by 20%: final = original × 1.25 × 0.80 = original × 1 = no change!"
- Alligation is the most underutilised shortcut. Teach it with mixtures first, then extend to averages problems

### Teaching Algebra
- Students fear quadratics — reframe as "finding the two numbers whose sum and product you know" (Vieta's)
- Inequalities: insist students draw number lines for every inequality problem

### Classroom Exercises
1. **Speed drill**: 20 quick percentage calculations in 5 minutes (use whiteboard relay)
2. **Option elimination**: Give a hard Quant question, cover the working — can they eliminate 2 options using estimation alone?
3. **Reverse engineering**: Give the answer, students work backward to find what the question must have been

## Common Mistakes (with Fixes)

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Calculating profit% on SP instead of CP | Always ask: "Profit % on what?" Default is CP in CAT |
| Using simple interest formula for CI problems | Identify keywords: "compound" → use multiplier (1+r)ⁿ |
| Forgetting to double-count in probability | Use complementary counting when direct counting is complex |
| Wrong application of relative speed | Draw a diagram: same direction → subtraction; opposite → addition |
| P&C: treating identical objects as distinct | Divide by the factorial of the count of each repeated item |
| Logarithm: forgetting base | In CAT, log without base = log₁₀. If base changes, use change-of-base formula |
| Geometry: area of triangle with just sides | Use Heron's formula — students forget to compute semi-perimeter first |

## Powerful Analogies

### Profit & Loss → Shopping with a Friend
Imagine you buy a shirt for ₹500 (CP) and your friend marks it at ₹700 (MP). He gives you a 25% discount. You pay ₹525. He made ₹25 profit = 5% on his cost. The "markup then discount" story makes this intuitive.

### Time & Work → Filling a Swimming Pool
Three friends each have a hose. Their individual rates add together. If one has a drainage pipe (negative rate), subtract. Total work = the pool's capacity. Always set capacity = LCM of the days to avoid fractions.

### AP/GP → Staircase vs Rocketship
An AP is a staircase — each step is the same height. A GP is a rocket — each step is a fixed multiple of the last. If someone's salary grows by ₹5000/year = AP. If it grows by 10%/year = GP (like compound interest).

### Probability → Drawing from a Magic Bag
Probability is just counting — "how many ways can I get what I want?" divided by "how many total ways?". Every probability problem is a counting problem in disguise.

### Logarithm → The Exponent-Finder
"log₂(8) = 3" means "what power of 2 gives me 8? Answer: 3." Logarithm is just the exponent. log is to multiplication what subtraction is to addition — they undo each other.
