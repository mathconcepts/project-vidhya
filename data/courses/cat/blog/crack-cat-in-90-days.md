# How to Crack CAT in 90 Days: The No-Nonsense Study Plan

*Published: March 2026 | Category: CAT Preparation | Reading time: 8 min*

---

You've decided: 90 days, CAT, IIM. Now what?

Most CAT advice tells you to "study smart, not hard." That's useless without specifics. This is the actual plan — what to do, week by week, based on how CAT has been designed over the last five years.

## First, Know What You're Actually Preparing For

CAT 2024 has three sections (each 40 minutes):
- **VARC** (24 questions): Reading Comprehension + Verbal Ability
- **DILR** (22 questions): Data Interpretation + Logical Reasoning  
- **QA** (22 questions): Quantitative Aptitude

MCQs: +3/−1. TITA (fill-in-the-blank): +3/no negative.

The target percentile for top IIMs is 99+. That sounds impossible. It isn't — it means getting roughly 48–54 questions correct out of 66 with high accuracy. About 73% accuracy on two-thirds of the paper.

That's achievable in 90 days with the right approach.

## The Three Mistakes That Sink CAT Aspirants

**Mistake 1: Treating CAT like a syllabus exam**

CAT isn't JEE or NEET. You can't memorise your way to a 99 percentile. The exam tests *applied reasoning under time pressure*. Students who over-invest in theory and under-invest in mocks consistently underperform.

Rule: After week 6, spend more time on mocks than concept study.

**Mistake 2: Equal time on all three sections**

Your time is not equally valuable across all sections. If you're strong in QA but weak in DILR, every additional QA practice hour returns less than a DILR hour. Map your strengths in week 1 by taking a diagnostic mock. Then allocate study time inversely to your current ability.

**Mistake 3: Doing mocks but not analysing them**

Taking 20 mocks without deep analysis is almost useless. Every mock should be followed by a 90-minute analysis session where you categorise every wrong answer:
- Conceptual error (didn't know the method)
- Application error (knew the method, made a mistake)
- Time management error (ran out of time, not difficulty)

Each error type needs a different fix. Conceptual → study. Application → drill. Time management → strategy adjustment.

## The 90-Day Framework

### Weeks 1–4: Foundation

**Quant (2 weeks)**: Arithmetic first (percentage, ratio, profit-loss, time-work, time-speed-distance). These 5 sub-topics account for 35–40% of QA questions. Then number systems and algebra. Geometry last.

*Target by week 4*: 14/22 correct in a timed QA mock.

**VARC (1 week)**: Para-jumbles, para-summary, odd sentence out. These are learnable with practice — unlike RC, which takes longer to develop. Get the low-hanging fruit first.

*Target by week 4*: 5/8 correct on VA section.

**DILR (1 week)**: Standard DI types (tables, bar charts, pie charts). Then basic LR (linear arrangement, grouping). Learn the drawing-first habit from day one — it saves time and errors.

*Target by week 4*: Able to fully solve 2 standard sets in 25 minutes.

### Weeks 5–8: Application

This is where most students plateau. The fix: **timed practice with immediate error classification**.

For VARC: Do 2 RC passages per day at exam speed. The goal isn't to finish them — it's to improve accuracy while maintaining pace. RC is the biggest VARC lever; improving RC accuracy from 60% to 80% can add 4–5 marks.

For DILR: The skill you're actually building here is **set selection** — knowing which sets to attempt and which to skip. Practice this explicitly. Before each mock, spend 2 minutes scanning all sets and ranking them. Your selection skill determines your DILR score more than your solving speed.

For QA: Weekly timed mock of 22 questions, full analysis. Identify your consistent weak topics and drill them specifically.

**Take your first full CAT mock in week 6.** Don't wait until you "feel ready" — no one feels ready. The diagnostic data is worth more than another week of prep.

### Weeks 9–12: Exam Mode

Two goals:
1. **Calibrate your attempt strategy**: How many questions should you attempt in each section? This is individual. Find your optimal attempt count through mock iteration.
2. **Eliminate avoidable errors**: Silly mistakes, unit errors, misread options — these are all fixable with checklists and awareness drills.

In this phase, theory study drops to almost zero. You're in maintenance + optimisation mode.

## Section-Specific Strategies

### Quant: The Formula Isn't the Hard Part

CAT QA questions are deceptively simple in setup but require elegant shortcuts to solve in 2 minutes. The students who score 95%+ in QA aren't faster calculators — they *recognise the pattern* and jump to the method.

How to build this: For every problem you solve, ask "What was the key insight?" Write it down. After 300 problems, you'll have a personalised shortcut library.

TITA questions in QA: these are typically cleaner problems with exact integer/fraction answers. Practice getting to the answer without multiple-choice elimination — it's a different skill.

### VARC: The Answer Is Always in the Passage

This is the most misunderstood section. Students read the passage, form an opinion, then answer based on that opinion. CAT RC doesn't care about your opinion.

The right approach: every answer must be traceable to specific lines in the passage. If you can't point to where the text supports your answer, reconsider.

For para-jumbles: CAT moved mostly to TITA format. This means no elimination strategy — you must actually order the sentences. The trick: find the mandatory first sentence (contains a new concept introduction, no pronoun reference, no connectors like "however/moreover/but" at start) and the mandatory last (conclusion or summary of the paragraph's argument).

### DILR: It's a Strategy Game

DILR separates 95th percentile from 99th percentile more than any other section. The reason: it rewards *smart selection* far more than raw ability.

The 6 sets in DILR are never equally hard. Typically 2 are accessible, 2 are medium, 2 are very hard. Your job in the first 2 minutes: identify the 2 accessible ones and solve them perfectly. Then attempt the medium ones.

Students who attempt all 6 sets partially consistently underperform students who solve 3 sets completely.

## The TITA Opportunity

With CAT's shift to more TITA questions (Type In The Answer, no negative marking), there's a reliable strategy: **always attempt TITA questions you've partially worked through**.

If you've done 2 out of 4 questions in a DILR set and the last question is TITA, attempt it. Your partial logic is already applied — even a 40% confidence guess returns +3 expected value (0.4 × 3 + 0.6 × 0 = 1.2 marks), which beats skipping (0 marks).

## The Final 3 Days

Day 3 before: Light mock (just 2 sections). Review your personal formula sheet. Sleep 8 hours.

Day 2 before: No mocks. Review your mistake log — what are the 5 errors you've made most often? Those are the ones to consciously avoid.

Day before: Nothing. Seriously. Lay out your exam kit, eat well, sleep early.

**Exam day**: You've done the work. Trust it.

The 90 days are the easy part — you just have to show up every day. The exam itself is just 2 hours. Go get it.
