# Reading Comprehension — CAT Lecture Notes

## What This Section Tests

RC is the largest component of the VARC section — roughly 16 questions out of 24, typically spread across 4-5 passages of 400-700 words each. CAT RC tests **three distinct cognitive skills**: (1) accurate comprehension of what the author says, (2) inference about what the author implies, and (3) evaluation of the author's tone, purpose, and logical structure.

Unlike many other exams, CAT RC passages are genuinely difficult — drawn from journals, high-quality newspapers, and academic writing. Topics span: philosophy, economics, history, sociology, literary criticism, science & technology, business and policy. You will rarely see a passage on a topic you studied in school. The difficulty is intentional: CAT tests language ability and reasoning, not domain knowledge.

## Core Concepts and Sub-Topics

### 1. Main Idea Questions
"What is the primary purpose of the passage?" or "Which of the following best describes the central argument?"
- The main idea is the **one claim the entire passage is building toward**
- It is never a detail, never an example, never the topic alone (topic ≠ main idea)
- Test: Does the main idea explain why all the paragraphs in the passage exist?

**How to find it**: After reading, ask: "If I had to defend this passage in one sentence to someone who hadn't read it, what would I say?"

### 2. Specific Detail / Factual Questions
"According to the passage..." or "The author mentions X in order to..."
- These have a specific textual location. Go back and find it.
- Never answer from memory — the trap is that a detail seems familiar but is subtly twisted in the option
- Keywords in the question → scan for those keywords in the passage → read 2-3 sentences around them

### 3. Inference Questions
"It can be inferred that..." or "The author would most likely agree that..."
- The answer is **not explicitly stated** but logically follows from what is stated
- Correct inference = true given the passage, supported by evidence in the passage, does not go beyond what the passage can reasonably imply
- Incorrect inferences: too extreme, external information, or only partially supported

### 4. Tone and Attitude Questions
"The author's tone toward X is best described as..."
- Common tone options: critical, analytical, appreciative, sceptical, ironic, dispassionate, nostalgic, cautious
- Look for **evaluative language** (positive/negative adjectives, hedging words like "ostensibly", "supposedly")
- Distinguish between the author's tone and a character's tone the author is describing

### 5. Vocabulary in Context
"As used in paragraph 3, the word 'X' most closely means..."
- Do not rely on dictionary definition — check how the word functions in the specific sentence
- The correct answer is what fits the sentence's logic, not just the most common meaning of the word

### 6. Structure Questions
"The second paragraph serves primarily to..."
- Options typically: introduce a counterargument, provide evidence, give an example, qualify the first claim, introduce a new concept
- Understand how each paragraph contributes to the overall argument

## Key CAT-Specific Strategies

### Pre-Reading: Set Up Your Mental Frame
Before reading, note the passage topic and your goal: understand the author's **central claim** and **attitude**. This prevents passive reading.

### Active Reading Technique
- **Paragraph-by-paragraph summary**: After each paragraph, pause and state its function in one clause ("this qualifies the claim", "this gives the historical context", "this introduces the counterargument")
- **Author tracker**: Always ask "what does the author think about this?" — many students read what is described but miss the author's evaluation

### Question Order Strategy
1. **Main idea** — answer this first or last (after reading the whole passage); never answer it mid-read
2. **Specific detail** — go back, scan, verify
3. **Inference** — requires the most careful elimination
4. **Tone** — judge from accumulation of language cues throughout the passage

### Passage Selection Strategy
If time is tight (and it will be), make passage selection a conscious choice:
- Read the first 2-3 sentences of each passage before committing
- Choose passages on topics where you find the author's argument easier to follow
- Avoid passages that front-load dense statistics or multi-step chains of causality in the opening paragraph — they take longer to parse

## Types of Passages and Their Structures

| Type | Typical Structure | Key Trap |
|------|-------------------|---------|
| Argumentative | Thesis → Evidence → Counterargument → Rebuttal | Students confuse author's view with presented counterargument |
| Descriptive | Topic → Sub-topics → Comparison | Main idea asked, but students cite a sub-topic |
| Analytical | Problem → Analysis → Implication | Inference questions ask about implications not explicitly stated |
| Narrative | Story → Reflection | Tone questions are subtle; events ≠ author's attitude |

## Common Traps and Mistakes

- **Choosing the "too extreme" option**: Options with "always", "never", "only", "completely" are usually wrong unless the passage uses such absolute language
- **Confusing the author's view with a view the author is critiquing**: Authors describe other views in detail before refuting them; students mistake the described view for the author's
- **Going outside the passage**: CAT RC is a closed-book test of reading comprehension — the answer must be in the passage or directly inferred from it
- **Main idea at the detail level**: Choosing an option that accurately describes paragraph 2 but misses what the whole passage argues
- **Tone inference from facts**: A passage can describe something terrible in a dispassionate, analytical tone; do not project emotional tone onto the author

## Time Management Tips

- **Per passage**: Allocate ~8-10 minutes for a passage with 4 questions (2 min reading, ~2 min per question)
- **Total VARC target**: 24 questions in 40 minutes means ~1.67 min/question — RC questions take more, VA questions less
- **Speed reading target**: 250-300 words per minute with comprehension (not skimming)
- **Never read an RC passage twice from scratch** — it is a time disaster; re-read only the specific section relevant to a question
- **The "3 wrong then guess" rule**: If you have eliminated 3 options and remain uncertain between two, commit; do not spiral
