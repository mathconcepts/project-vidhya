# Teaching Tips — DILR (CAT)

## For Students

### The One Truth About DILR
DILR is the only CAT section where raw speed doesn't save you — **set selection does**. The student who correctly identifies the 3 easiest sets and solves them completely will outscore the student who attempts all 6 sets partially.

### Before You Solve Anything — Survey the Section
In the first 2–3 minutes of DILR, read the first line of every set. Classify each:
- ✅ "I know this format" (arrangements, standard tables)
- ⚠️ "Looks manageable but complex" (multi-constraint grouping)
- ❌ "Novel/weird/never seen this" (unfamiliar format, skip for now)

Attempt in order: ✅ → ⚠️ → ❌ (only if time remains)

### The Drawing Rule
**Never solve DILR in your head.** Ever. Draw:
- Arrangements → boxes on paper
- Grouping → constraint table
- DI → extract numbers to your own mini-table before calculating

Students who draw make 60% fewer errors than those who don't.

### DI Speed Techniques

**Approximate first, verify if needed**: CAT DI options are usually spread 3–5% apart. Calculate to 1–2 significant figures first. Only go precise if two options are close.

**Unit check ritual**: Before the first calculation, circle the units in the data source. "₹ Crore", "Thousands", "Per Capita" — unit errors are the #1 source of DI mistakes.

**Table extraction**: For caselet DI (data given as paragraph), **stop reading and build a table first**. The time to build it is paid back 3× in faster question solving.

### LR Constraint Ordering
When you have 6 constraints for an arrangement:
1. **Absolute first**: "A sits at position 1" → fix it
2. **Definite relative**: "B sits immediately right of C" → two cells locked together
3. **Negative constraints last**: "D is not adjacent to A" → eliminates possibilities after structure is set

Never start with negative constraints — you'll spiral.

### The TITA Opportunity
TITA (Type In The Answer) questions have NO negative marking. In a 4-question set:
- If 2 are MCQ and 2 are TITA — always attempt the TITA even with 50% confidence
- If you've correctly solved 2 questions in a set, your logic is likely right — the TITA answer follows

### Analogies That Help
- DILR is like **assembling IKEA furniture**: you have instructions (constraints), pieces (entities), and a target picture (the valid arrangement). Draw the table/grid first — that's your IKEA diagram. Without it, you're assembling blindly.
- Set selection is like **buffet strategy**: you scan all dishes first, pile up what you love, and skip what you've never tasted. Never spend 10 minutes on the weird dish when there are 3 favourites waiting.

## For Teachers / Tutors

### What Makes DILR Hard
DILR is hard for two distinct reasons that require different fixes:
1. **Format unfamiliarity** — student has never seen "circular arrangement with dual constraint" → fix with exposure (practice 2 sets/day)
2. **Panic under time pressure** — student knows the logic but freezes → fix with timed sets (30 minutes, 6 sets, strict stopwatch)

Identify which problem your student has before deciding the intervention.

### Teaching Sequence (4-week plan)
**Week 1**: DI only — tables, bar charts, pie charts, caselets. Drill approximation techniques.

**Week 2**: LR basics — linear arrangement, circular arrangement, grouping. Constraint ordering drills.

**Week 3**: LR advanced — scheduling, blood relations, set theory, direction. Mixed practice sets.

**Week 4**: Full DILR mock sets under timed conditions. Set selection strategy. Post-mock analysis.

### High-Leverage Teaching Moments

**The "Abandon Early" Lesson**
Most students resist abandoning a set they've invested 5 minutes in. Run this exercise: give a deliberately hard set (complex puzzle with many constraints). After 5 minutes with no solution, explicitly say "stop, move on." Then debrief: "Those 5 minutes on 0 marks could have been 6 marks on an easier set." Repeat until set-abandonment becomes instinctive.

**Live Drawing Demonstration**
Solve an arrangement on the board while thinking aloud: "I'm drawing 6 boxes for 6 positions. Constraint 1 says A is at position 3 — I write A in box 3. Constraint 2 says B is immediately right of A — B goes in box 4..." This models the *process*, not just the answer.

**The Approximation Challenge**
Give students a DI question and ask: "Solve it using approximation in 45 seconds." Then show exact calculation. If the approximated answer is within the correct option's range, they've just cut 60 seconds of calculation. Do this for 10 questions until it's habitual.

### Common Student Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---------------|---------|
| "I need to attempt all 6 sets to score well" | 3 perfect sets ≈ 12 marks = 90%+ DILR percentile |
| "I'm bad at maths so DI will be hard" | CAT DI is approximation-heavy, not calculation-heavy |
| "LR is random — you either get it or you don't" | LR is deterministic — every valid arrangement follows from constraints. If stuck, you missed a constraint |
| "I should always start with the easiest question in a set" | In LR, Q1 is often setup; Q2/Q3 are often easier once Q1 is solved |

### Assessment Checkpoints
| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| Beginner | Can solve a standard table DI + basic linear arrangement |
| Intermediate | Correctly attempts 4 sets in 45 min; abandons hard sets |
| Advanced | 14+ correct in 40 minutes; set selection in first 2 min |
| CAT-ready | 16–20 correct in 40 min; handles novel set types; TITA attempts |
